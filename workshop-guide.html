Facilitator Guide

A step-by-step guide to running the Affinity Map and Lightning Decision Jam sessions. Follow the steps in order — everything you need is here.

⏱ ~75 min total 👥 4–10 participants 🖥 Miro / Zoom 📌 Sticky notes
Phase 1 · ~30 min

Affinity Map

Participants surface the problems they care about. Similar problems are clustered together to reveal shared interests and emerging themes — making invisible patterns visible to the whole group.

Phase 2 · ~45 min

Lightning Decision Jam

The group moves from shared understanding to structured action — prioritising one problem, generating solutions, and identifying concrete next steps.

Phase 1 · Affinity Map
Step 1 of 5

Set the Question

⚙️ Setup — before participants join
Objective

Frame a clear, open-ended question that invites participants to share one specific problem they want to work on — not an idea, not a solution, just a problem.

1
Prepare the canvas

Open your Miro board. Create a large open area with a guiding question written at the top, clearly visible to all participants.

2
Write the guiding question

Display this question for participants:

"What is one problem related to Agentic AI that you would like to work on today?"

3
Add a sticky note example

Place one example sticky note nearby so participants understand the expected format: short, specific, written as a problem — not a solution or general topic.

📌 Facilitator Note

The Affinity Map technique was created by Jiro Kawakita in the 1960s (also known as the KJ Method). Its effectiveness depends on two things: generating multiple quality inputs, and clean sorting that reveals genuine relationships. Keep the question narrow — one problem per person, not a brainstorm.

Step 2 of 5

Silent Problem Writing

⏱ 10 minutes
Objective

Each participant independently writes down one problem they would like to work on — in silence, without influence from others.

1
Read the question aloud

Point participants to the guiding question on the board. Make sure everyone can see it.

2
Instruct participants — silent, individual writing

Ask each participant to take 10 minutes to write their response on a sticky note. This is done in silence — no discussion, no sharing yet. For groups of four or fewer, index cards work well instead of sticky notes.

3
One problem per person

Remind participants: write one problem only — something specific and real to them. Not a broad topic, not a solution.

📌 Facilitator Note

Silence is important here. It prevents participants from anchoring on each other's ideas and ensures every voice — not just the most vocal — is represented in the output. Do not rush this step.

Post & Display

⏱ 5 minutes
Objective

Collect all sticky notes and make them visible to the entire group on a shared surface.

1
Collect all sticky notes simultaneously

Ask participants to post their sticky note(s) on the shared Miro board at the same time. This avoids anyone being put on the spot.

2
Keep them visible to everyone

Arrange notes loosely on a flat working surface (the Miro canvas) so the whole group can read them. No discussion yet.

3
Note duplicates — don't remove them

If two participants wrote similar problems, keep both. Duplicates are valuable — they indicate shared concerns and group consensus.

Step 4 of 5

Sort & Cluster

⏱ 10 minutes
Objective

Group similar problems together into natural clusters, making patterns and shared interests visible to the whole group.

1
Invite participants to start sorting

Based on guidance from the group, begin sorting sticky notes into columns or clusters based on their relationships and similarities.

2
Encourage physical engagement

Ask participants to actively engage with the Miro board — moving notes, suggesting groupings, and performing preliminary clustering themselves. Don't let the facilitator do all the work.

3
Create a parking lot

Create a sticky-note "parking lot" area nearby for any notes that don't fit a natural category. These can be revisited later or remain ungrouped.

📌 Facilitator Note

Don't over-engineer the clusters. Let natural groupings emerge from the content rather than imposing categories in advance. It's fine to have uneven cluster sizes — some themes will simply attract more problems than others.

Step 5 of 5

Name the Categories

⏱ 5 minutes
Objective

Give each cluster a short, descriptive label that the group agrees on — transforming raw problems into named, shared themes.

1
Ask the group to suggest category names

Once the content is sorted, ask participants to suggest a name for each cluster. The name should capture what all the problems in that group have in common.

2
Label each cluster

Place a labelling sticky note (different colour or larger size) at the top of each cluster with the agreed category name.

3
Don't get stuck on naming

Avoid prolonged naming debates. A good-enough name is better than a perfect name debated for five minutes. Move on once there's rough agreement.

📌 Facilitator Note

At the end of this step, briefly show the full map to the group and read out the category names. This is the "aha" moment — participants often notice they share concerns they didn't know others had. This shared awareness is what makes the next phase (LDJ) more focused and productive.

Affinity Map · Explore & Sense-Make → LDJ · Converge & Decide
📌 Facilitator Note

Use the Affinity Map clusters as the starting context when you open the LDJ. You don't need to re-explain everything — just remind participants: "We've seen what problems are on people's minds. Now let's work through one of them together using a structured process."

Phase 2 · Lightning Decision Jam
LDJ Step 1 of 8

Start on a Positive Note

⏱ 10 minutes
Objective

Begin with what's working well. This sets a constructive tone before moving into problems and challenges.

🗺 Sailboat canvas 📌 Sticky notes 🖊 Markers
The Sailboat Canvas — top half for strengths, bottom half for challenges.
1
Prepare the Sailboat Canvas

Create a large square on the Miro board divided by a wavy horizontal line. The top half (with a sail) represents strengths. The bottom half (with an anchor) represents challenges.

2
Frame the session topic briefly

Remind the group of the workshop focus. Then ask participants to silently write what is working well — one item per sticky note. 4 minutes, in silence.

3
Each participant shares their positives

Each person reads out their sticky notes (1–2 minutes each) and places them on the top half of the sailboat. No interruptions — only clarifying questions allowed.

📌 Facilitator Note

These notes are not carried forward — they serve their purpose after being shared. Do not let this step run over time. Move on once everyone has presented.

LDJ Step 2 of 8

Capture Problems

⏱ 5 minutes
Objective

Give everyone space to write down the problems, frustrations, and concerns on their mind — silently and without judgement.

1
4 minutes of silent writing

All participants spend 4 minutes silently writing problems, frustrations, mistakes, or concerns on individual sticky notes. Encourage generating as many as possible — quantity matters here.

2
Post all at once — no reading aloud

After 4 minutes, everyone posts their sticky notes simultaneously onto the bottom half of the sailboat canvas (1 minute). Notes are not read aloud.

📌 Facilitator Note

Posting simultaneously avoids putting anyone on the spot. Participants don't need to justify or explain their problems at this stage — that pressure kills honesty. Let the sticky notes speak for themselves.

Phase 2 · Lightning Decision Jam
LDJ Step 3 of 8

Prioritize Problems

⏱ 3 minutes
Objective

Use democratic dot voting to surface the most important problems — without discussion or debate.

🟣 3 dot stickers per person
1
Give each participant 3 dot stickers

Each person gets exactly 3 votes (dots). In Miro, use the voting feature or ask participants to place emoji dots on the sticky notes they choose.

2
Vote individually — no discussion

Voting happens in silence. Participants can vote on their own sticky notes. They can also place multiple votes on a single item they feel strongly about.

3
Arrange by vote count

After voting, the facilitator arranges problems in priority order — most votes at the top. This typically forms a triangle or "Christmas tree" shape.

📌 Facilitator Note

Voting is far better than open conversation for this — it prevents dominant voices from driving the agenda and ensures every participant's view is counted equally. Don't skip it or shortcut it with discussion.

Phase 2 · Lightning Decision Jam
LDJ Step 4 of 8

Reframe as "How Might We"

⏱ 3 minutes
Objective

Transform the top-voted problem into an open, actionable challenge question using the "How Might We" (HMW) format.

1
Take the top-voted problem

Select the problem with the most dot votes. This becomes the focus of the rest of the session.

2
Reframe it as a HMW question

Write it as: "How Might We [solve the problem]?"

The HMW format works because:
HOW — assumes action is possible
MIGHT — acknowledges multiple solutions exist
WE — frames it as a collaborative effort

3
Display it prominently

Write the HMW question large and clearly on the board where everyone can see it throughout the rest of the session.

📌 Facilitator Note

You can optionally repeat this step for up to 4–5 top-voted problems if time allows. Lower-voted problems can be placed in a backlog for future sessions. For this workshop, focus on one HMW to keep momentum.

Phase 2 · Lightning Decision Jam
LDJ Step 5 of 8

Ideate Solutions

⏱ 6 minutes
Objective

Generate as many potential solutions to the HMW question as possible — in silence, prioritising quantity over quality.

1
Display the HMW question

Make sure the "How Might We" question is clearly visible to everyone on the board.

2
5 minutes of silent idea generation

Participants write as many potential solutions as possible — one idea per sticky note. Emphasise: "Quantity over quality." Aim for breadth, not refinement.

3
Ideas must stand alone

Each sticky note must be understandable when read on its own — without verbal explanation. If it needs explaining, it needs rewriting.

4
Post all at once — no discussion

After 5 minutes, everyone places their sticky notes on the working area simultaneously (1 minute). No presenting, no explaining.

Prioritize Solutions

⏱ 5 minutes
Objective

Use a second round of dot voting to identify the solutions the group finds most promising.

🟢 6 dot stickers per person
1
Give each participant 6 dot stickers

Each person gets 6 votes for this round — more than the problem-voting round to reflect the larger number of ideas.

2
4 minutes to vote on solutions

Vote on the solutions that best address the HMW question. Vote silently and individually. You can distribute votes however you like.

3
Reorganise by vote count

After voting, the facilitator reorganises solutions by number of votes. Solutions with only one vote are typically set aside.

Effort / Impact Scale

⏱ 10 minutes
Objective

Plot the top-voted solutions on a 2×2 matrix to identify which are high-impact and low-effort — the "sweet spot" to act on immediately.

← Less effort More effort →
Top-Left ⭐ Sweet Spot
High Impact · Low Effort
The priority zone. Execute these immediately.
→ Act now
Top-Right
High Impact · High Effort
Worth doing — plan as larger projects.
→ Plan for later
Bottom-Left
Low Impact · Low Effort
Quick wins — optional to pursue.
→ Optional
Bottom-Right
Low Impact · High Effort
Avoid. Not worth the effort right now.
→ Shelve
↑ High impact Low impact ↓
1
Draw the Effort/Impact grid

Create a square on the Miro board with two axes: horizontal = Effort (less → more), vertical = Impact (not at all → completely). Draw lines through the centre creating four quadrants.

2
Place each top-voted solution on the grid

Take the highest-voted solutions one at a time. For each one, ask the group:
— "Is the impact higher or lower?" → move it vertically
— "Is the effort higher or lower?" → move it horizontally
Keep each discussion under 20 seconds. Approximations are fine.

3
Mark the sweet spot

Solutions landing in the top-left quadrant (high impact, low effort) are your sweet spot. Mark these with a contrasting coloured dot or star.

📌 Facilitator Note

Don't let discussions drag on. The 20-second rule is strict by design — fast decisions prevent over-analysis. The exact position on the grid is less important than identifying which quadrant a solution belongs to.

Step 12 / 14
LDJ Step 7
Phase 2 · Lightning Decision Jam
LDJ Step 8 of 8

Make It Actionable

⏱ 5 minutes
Objective

Turn sweet-spot solutions into concrete, assigned action steps that can begin immediately — not someday.

1
Focus on sweet-spot solutions

Take each solution from the top-left (sweet spot) quadrant. For each, the team develops three action steps to test the solution.

2
Define the first step — make it frictionless

The first action step should be easy and completable almost immediately. If the first step requires approval, budget, or preparation, it's too big — break it down further.

3
Keep action steps short in scope

All action steps should be completable within 1–2 weeks maximum. Document each step on rectangular sticky notes placed next to the solution.

4
Assign a responsible person

Each solution must be assigned to one team member who takes ownership. No shared ownership — one name per solution.

5
Capture and share the results

Take a screenshot or export the Miro board. Share with all participants immediately after the session.

📌 Facilitator Note

After the workshop, schedule a short 10-minute check-in with each responsible person after 1–2 weeks to discuss progress, current work, and any blockers. This follow-up is what turns workshop outputs into real change.

🎉

Workshop Complete

The group has moved from a shared map of problems to a set of prioritised, actionable next steps. Well done.
